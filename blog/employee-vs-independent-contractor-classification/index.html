Employee vs. Independent Contractor: Misclassification Risks Employers Must Know

By Michael A. Zara, Esq. | June 25, 2026

Schedule a Consultation (866) 823-8288
By Michael A. Zara, Esq. | June 25, 2026 | Employment Law

The decision to engage a worker as an independent contractor rather than an employee is one of the most consequential legal choices a growing business makes, and one of the most commonly made incorrectly. Misclassification is not a paperwork technicality. It triggers liability for back payroll taxes, unpaid overtime, benefits owed, workers' compensation exposure, and state penalties that can compound into six or seven figures for businesses with even a handful of workers classified improperly. Enforcement has intensified at both the federal and state levels, and the label you put on a working relationship in a contract is not what determines classification: the actual economic and operational reality of the relationship is what counts.

Understanding how classification is evaluated, where the risk concentrates, and how to structure contractor relationships that will hold up to scrutiny is essential for any business that relies on flexible workforce arrangements.

Why Classification Matters More Than You Think

The financial stakes of misclassification are substantial and multi-layered. When a worker is reclassified from contractor to employee, the employer becomes liable for the employer's share of FICA taxes that were never withheld, along with interest and penalties on those amounts. The IRS can assess a penalty equal to the employee's share of FICA as well if the employer failed to withhold it. Federal unemployment taxes (FUTA) that were never paid come due. If the worker was entitled to overtime under the Fair Labor Standards Act, unpaid overtime claims go back two years, or three years if the violation was willful.

State liability compounds this. Most states impose their own payroll taxes and workers' compensation requirements. Many have their own minimum wage and overtime laws with separate penalty structures. A handful of states, including California, New Jersey, and Massachusetts, apply a strict three-part test that makes independent contractor classification far harder to sustain than the federal standard, with civil and criminal penalties for violations.

Beyond taxes, misclassified workers may bring wage and hour claims, demands for retroactive benefits they would have received as employees, and claims under anti-discrimination laws that apply to employees but not to independent contractors. Class action litigation in this space has grown significantly, particularly in industries like technology, logistics, healthcare staffing, and construction. The operational flexibility you gained by using contractors can evaporate quickly when retroactive liability attaches across an entire workforce.

The Legal Tests: There Is No Single Standard

One of the most disorienting features of worker classification law is that there is no single test that applies across all contexts. Different agencies and different legal frameworks apply different standards, and satisfying one does not guarantee compliance with another.

The IRS Common Law Test focuses on behavioral control, financial control, and the type of relationship. Under behavioral control, the question is whether the business has the right to direct how the work is done, not just what result is delivered. Providing training, setting specific work hours, requiring work to be performed on company premises, and dictating the sequence of tasks all indicate employment. Under financial control, the question is whether the worker has a meaningful investment in their own tools, bears a risk of profit or loss, and performs services for multiple businesses rather than working exclusively for one client. The type of relationship prong looks at whether there is a written contract, whether the worker receives benefits, and whether the relationship is permanent or project-specific.

The FLSA's Economic Reality Test governs federal wage and hour law. Courts applying this test ask whether the worker is economically dependent on the employer or genuinely in business for themselves. The factors considered include the degree of the employer's control over the work, the worker's opportunity for profit or loss, the worker's investment in facilities or equipment, whether the work requires special skill, the permanence of the relationship, and the degree to which the work is integral to the employer's regular business operations. A worker who performs core business functions, works exclusively for one company, uses that company's equipment, and follows its procedures is almost certainly an employee under this standard regardless of how the contract is labeled.

The ABC Test, applied in California, Massachusetts, New Jersey, and a growing number of other states, is substantially more demanding. Under the ABC test, a worker is presumed to be an employee unless the hiring business can affirmatively establish all three of the following: (A) the worker is free from the control and direction of the hiring entity in connection with performing the work; (B) the worker performs work that is outside the usual course of the hiring entity's business; and (C) the worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, or business of the same nature as the work performed. The second prong is where many businesses fail. If you are a software company and you engage a developer to build features for your product, that developer is performing work squarely within your usual course of business. Under the ABC test, they are almost certainly your employee regardless of any contractor agreement.

Common Industries at Elevated Risk

While misclassification risk exists across sectors, certain industries carry elevated exposure due to the nature of how their workforces are structured.

Technology companies routinely engage developers, designers, and marketing specialists as contractors. The problem is that much of this work is integral to the company's core business, the workers often work on-site or in tight coordination with internal teams, and the engagement periods are long enough to look like employment in practice. Technology businesses are among the most frequently audited in this space and should review contractor arrangements carefully, particularly where the contractor works full-time hours exclusively for one company.

Professional services firms, including consulting practices, staffing firms, and creative agencies, often rely heavily on contractor networks to deliver client work. When contractors are billed out to clients as part of the firm's deliverables and operate under the firm's direction in performing that client work, the line between contractor and employee blurs quickly. Professional services businesses should pay particular attention to financial control factors: if a contractor is not pricing their own engagements, not bearing any risk of loss, and not working for any other clients, the economic reality of the relationship is employment.

Construction has a long history of misclassification exposure. Subcontractor arrangements that are legitimate in form can become employment relationships in practice when a general contractor exercises detailed control over how, when, and with what tools the subcontractor performs work on a project. Workers' compensation liability is a particular concern in this industry given the physical nature of the work.

Healthcare staffing, including home health, hospice, and locum tenens arrangements, faces increasing scrutiny from both the IRS and state labor agencies. The question of whether a per-diem nurse or traveling therapist is a contractor or an employee of the staffing agency, the healthcare facility, or both, involves multiple layers of analysis that require legal guidance.

What a Defensible Contractor Relationship Looks Like

Labeling a worker a contractor and having them sign an independent contractor agreement is not sufficient to establish or protect that classification. The substance of the relationship must support the label. There are concrete steps businesses can take to structure contractor engagements in ways that reflect genuine contractor status.

Define the scope of work by outcome, not process. A true independent contractor delivers a defined result and determines on their own how to achieve it. Your contract should specify deliverables, deadlines, and acceptance criteria. It should not specify work hours, require attendance at internal meetings, dictate the specific methods the contractor must use, or prohibit the contractor from working for others.

Do not integrate the contractor into your workforce as though they were an employee. Contractors should not receive employee badges, be listed in your internal directory, participate in employee training programs, use company-provided equipment as a matter of standard practice, or be supervised by your managers in the same way your employees are supervised. Each of these integration factors weighs toward employee status in any of the classification tests.

Allow and expect the contractor to work for other clients. A contractor who works exclusively for your business, for extended periods, on work central to your business is presenting a risk profile that looks like an employment relationship from every regulatory angle. Genuine contractors typically maintain a portfolio of clients and are not economically dependent on any single one.

Use a well-drafted independent contractor agreement. While a contract alone cannot determine classification, a poorly drafted agreement can actively undermine your position. A solid contractor agreement, reviewed by experienced contract counsel, should clearly define the contractor's independent status, specify that the contractor is responsible for their own taxes and insurance, clarify that the contractor provides their own tools and equipment, limit the work to a defined project or scope, and include appropriate IP assignment and confidentiality provisions. It should not contain provisions that look like employee handbook language, performance review processes, or termination-at-will provisions that mirror an employment relationship.

Ensure the contractor has their own business structure. A worker who has formed their own LLC or corporation, carries their own general liability insurance, and maintains their own business accounts presents a stronger contractor profile than one who simply receives 1099s without any independent business infrastructure. When helping clients evaluate contractor arrangements, one of the first questions we ask is whether the contractor is actually set up to operate as a business. If they are not, the relationship is more likely to be characterized as disguised employment. Our business formation practice can assist contractors who need to establish a proper entity structure.

What to Do If You Suspect You Have a Misclassification Problem

Discovering a potential misclassification issue in your workforce is serious, but the response to that discovery matters enormously. Doing nothing is rarely the right answer. Unaddressed misclassification tends to compound: the longer it continues, the greater the retroactive liability that accumulates, and the worse the position a business is in if an audit or a worker complaint triggers an investigation.

At the same time, simply reclassifying contractors as employees wholesale, without careful analysis, can itself create problems. Reclassification is a fact-intensive process that should be evaluated on a worker-by-worker or category-by-category basis. In some cases, the existing arrangement can be restructured to achieve genuine contractor status rather than reclassified into employment. In other cases, converting to employment is the correct outcome and should be handled in a way that minimizes disruption and retroactive liability exposure.

The IRS Voluntary Classification Settlement Program (VCSP) provides a mechanism for businesses to proactively resolve federal payroll tax misclassification issues at a significantly reduced cost compared to what an audit would produce. Eligibility and strategy for the VCSP should be evaluated with experienced employment counsel before any submission is made.

State voluntary disclosure programs are available in some jurisdictions as well, and the right combination of federal and state resolution pathways depends on the specific facts of each situation.

The Bottom Line for Business Owners

The business rationale for using independent contractors is clear: flexibility, reduced benefits costs, and the ability to scale without adding permanent headcount. Those benefits are real and available, but they require getting the legal foundation right. A contractor arrangement that does not reflect an actual contractor relationship is not a legitimate business arrangement; it is deferred liability that will eventually come due, often at a cost far exceeding what proper employment would have required from the outset.

The businesses we see with the most serious misclassification exposure are almost never the ones that set out to cut corners. They are typically businesses that grew quickly, relied on informal arrangements that worked in the early days, and never paused to evaluate whether those arrangements still made legal sense as the company scaled. A workforce audit conducted proactively, before a government agency or a plaintiff's attorney makes it necessary, is one of the most cost-effective legal investments a growing company can make.
