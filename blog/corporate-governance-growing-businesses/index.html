Corporate Governance for Growing Businesses: Boards, Bylaws, and Protecting Your Company

By Michael A. Zara, Esq. | July 2, 2026

Schedule a Consultation (866) 823-8288
By Michael A. Zara, Esq. | July 2, 2026 | Corporate Governance

When most business owners think about corporate governance, they picture boardrooms full of executives at Fortune 500 companies debating shareholder resolutions. That mental image causes many growing businesses to dismiss governance as irrelevant to their stage, and that dismissal is one of the more consequential mistakes a company can make. The governance structures that feel like bureaucratic overhead when a business has five employees become the frameworks that determine whether a business survives a co-founder dispute, attracts outside capital, completes a successful acquisition, or holds together through a leadership transition.

The businesses that wait until a crisis materializes to build governance structures are the ones that pay the highest price. By the time a majority owner and a minority investor are in open conflict, or a key partner is claiming rights no written document supports, or an acquirer's due diligence team discovers that corporate records have not been maintained for years, the cost of getting governance right has compounded dramatically. Building it correctly from the beginning, or correcting it during a period of relative calm, is far less expensive.

What Corporate Governance Actually Means for a Private Business

Corporate governance is the system of rules, practices, and processes by which a company is directed and controlled. For a publicly traded company, this includes compliance with SEC regulations, stock exchange listing requirements, and the expectations of institutional shareholders. For a private business, the governance framework is more flexible but no less important, and the consequences of getting it wrong can be just as severe.

At its core, private company governance answers a set of fundamental questions: Who has the authority to make which decisions? How are disputes between owners resolved? What happens when a co-founder or key shareholder wants to exit? Under what circumstances can the company take on debt, issue new equity, or sell the business? What records must be kept and how must they be maintained? How are profits distributed and on what timeline?

When these questions are answered clearly in founding documents, operating agreements, and governance policies, the business runs on rules rather than on relationships and assumptions. When they are not answered, every decision that touches on ownership, authority, or money becomes a potential dispute.

Our corporate governance practice works with businesses at every stage to establish governance frameworks that reflect the actual complexity of the company and hold up under pressure.

The Governing Documents That Every Business Needs

The specific documents that govern a business depend on its legal structure, but every entity type has foundational documents that define how the organization operates. These documents are not formalities to be filed and forgotten. They are the operating manual for the business, and they need to be drafted carefully, maintained accurately, and updated as the company evolves.

For corporations, the primary governing documents are the articles of incorporation (filed with the state), the bylaws (adopted by the board), and any shareholder agreements. The articles establish the company's legal existence and set certain fundamental parameters, including the authorized classes and number of shares. The bylaws govern day-to-day operations: how the board is elected, how meetings are called and conducted, what constitutes a quorum, what decisions require board versus shareholder approval, and how officers are appointed and removed. A shareholder agreement supplements these documents by addressing equity transfer restrictions, buy-sell provisions, voting arrangements, and dispute resolution mechanisms among specific owners.

For LLCs, the operating agreement serves the combined function of bylaws and shareholder agreement. In many states, an operating agreement is technically optional, but operating without one is a serious mistake. Without a tailored operating agreement, the company falls back on the state's default LLC statutes, which were designed for generic situations and rarely reflect the actual arrangements and intentions of the company's members. A well-drafted LLC operating agreement covers member contributions and percentage interests, how profits and losses are allocated, how distributions are made and when, what decisions require member approval versus manager authority, how new members can be admitted, and how a member can exit the company or transfer their interest.

These documents are only as valuable as they are current and accurate. A common governance failure we see in growing businesses is that the founding documents were drafted adequately at the start but were never updated to reflect subsequent equity issuances, changes in management structure, amendments to member agreements, or the addition of new investors. When a company's governance documents do not match its actual ownership and operational structure, that gap creates legal uncertainty that surfaces at the worst possible moments: during a financing round, a sale process, or a dispute.

Working with an attorney experienced in business entity structuring at the outset, and revisiting governing documents whenever the company's ownership or structure materially changes, is one of the most cost-effective governance investments a business can make.

Board Structure: When You Need One and How to Build It

Corporations are legally required to have a board of directors, but the size, composition, and function of that board can vary widely. LLCs are not required to have boards, but manager-managed LLCs often function with a governance structure that resembles one in practice.

For a small, owner-operated company, the board may consist entirely of the founders, function with minimal formality, and serve primarily as a compliance mechanism to maintain corporate formalities. As a company grows, particularly when it takes on outside investors or brings in non-founder management, the board typically evolves into a more substantive governance body that provides oversight, strategic counsel, and accountability for management.

When to consider adding outside directors. A board composed entirely of insiders, founders, and management has inherent limitations. Every director has the same perspective and the same interests. Adding independent directors, board members who are not employees or equity owners with significant economic stakes, provides external accountability and brings expertise that is often missing inside a growing company. An independent director with relevant industry experience or financial expertise can contribute genuine strategic value. More importantly, a board with independent directors provides a governance structure that outside investors, acquirers, and lenders recognize as credible and take seriously.

Advisory boards versus governing boards. Some companies find it useful to distinguish between a formal board of directors, which has actual fiduciary duties and legal decision-making authority, and an advisory board, which provides counsel without governance authority or liability. Advisory boards are easier to assemble because they do not carry the legal responsibilities of directorship, and they can be an effective way to attract experienced advisors who might be unwilling to take on the fiduciary duties of a formal board seat. The distinction matters, however: an advisory board cannot replace the governance function of an actual board of directors, and conflating the two creates confusion about who actually has authority to bind the company.

Board mechanics that matter. Beyond composition, the practical operation of the board determines how effective it is as a governance structure. Boards should meet on a defined schedule, with proper notice, and with minutes recorded and maintained. Decisions that the governing documents designate as board-level decisions must actually be taken by the board and documented. When major transactions are under consideration, whether a significant debt financing, an acquisition, an equity issuance, or a sale of the company, the board's deliberation and approval process should be documented contemporaneously. This documentation matters both for corporate integrity and for any future due diligence review.

Fiduciary Duties: What Officers and Directors Owe the Company

Every director and officer of a corporation owes fiduciary duties to the company and its shareholders. Understanding these duties is essential both for the individuals who hold these roles and for the shareholders who rely on them.

The duty of care requires directors and officers to act with the care that a reasonably prudent person in a similar position would exercise under similar circumstances. In practice, this means making decisions based on adequate information, taking sufficient time to deliberate on significant matters, seeking expert advice when appropriate, and attending and actively participating in board meetings. A director who rubber-stamps management decisions without independent review, or who is chronically absent from board deliberations, may fail the duty of care regardless of how the decisions ultimately turn out.

The duty of loyalty requires directors and officers to act in the interests of the company rather than in their own personal interests or the interests of another party. Conflicts of interest are common in private companies, particularly when directors or officers have relationships with vendors, customers, or investors who also deal with the company. The existence of a conflict does not automatically make a transaction improper, but it requires disclosure and typically requires that the conflicted party recuse themselves from the relevant decision. Failing to disclose and properly manage conflicts of interest is one of the most common triggers for derivative litigation by shareholders against directors and officers.

The business judgment rule provides directors significant protection from personal liability for business decisions, as long as those decisions were made on an informed basis, in good faith, and in the honest belief that the decision was in the best interests of the company. This protection is meaningful but not unlimited. It requires that the board actually followed a defensible decision-making process, not just that the outcome was reasonable in hindsight.

For LLC managers, similar duties typically apply, though the specific formulation depends on state law and the terms of the operating agreement. Some states permit LLC members to modify or limit fiduciary duties in the operating agreement, subject to a baseline of good faith and fair dealing. Understanding what duties apply in your specific entity and jurisdiction is an area where legal guidance from our corporate governance team can prevent costly disputes.

Protecting Minority Shareholders and Members

Minority interest holders in private companies face a structural vulnerability that their counterparts in publicly traded companies do not: there is no liquid market for their interest. A minority shareholder in a public company who disagrees with management can sell their shares. A minority member of a private LLC who has been frozen out of distributions, excluded from information, or oppressed by majority decisions often has no exit and no ready remedy without litigation.

The most effective protection for minority shareholders and members is contractual: governance documents drafted to include specific minority protections rather than relying on state law defaults. Common minority protections include information rights (the right to receive financial statements and inspect company records), anti-dilution provisions that protect the minority's percentage interest when new equity is issued, consent rights over fundamental transactions (sale of the company, major debt financings, changes to the governance structure), and tag-along rights that allow minority holders to participate on the same terms if the majority sells its interest.

Buy-sell agreements are another critical minority protection mechanism. A buy-sell agreement establishes a pre-agreed mechanism for valuing and transferring interests when a triggering event occurs, such as the death, disability, or departure of a co-owner, or a fundamental disagreement among owners. Without a buy-sell agreement, these events lead to protracted valuation disputes and, frequently, litigation. A well-drafted buy-sell agreement negotiated in good faith by all parties before any triggering event occurs is far more likely to produce a fair and workable outcome than a court proceeding.

Negotiating these protections as part of the foundational agreements that govern the business relationship is far easier than trying to obtain them after a dispute has arisen and the parties are adversarial.

Maintaining Corporate Formalities: Why It Matters More Than You Think

The legal separation between a business entity and its owners is one of the most valuable protections business law provides. When properly maintained, this separation means that the personal assets of the business owners are not at risk for the debts and liabilities of the business. When the separation is not maintained, courts can pierce the corporate veil and hold owners personally liable for business obligations.

Maintaining corporate formalities is the primary mechanism by which this legal separation is preserved. The specific requirements vary by entity type and state, but the core practices are consistent: hold required meetings (or take actions by written consent in lieu of meetings), document board and shareholder decisions in minutes or resolutions, keep separate bank accounts and financial records for the business, avoid commingling personal and business funds, ensure that contracts and agreements are signed in the entity's name rather than by the individual owner in their personal capacity, and file required annual reports and pay required fees to maintain the entity in good standing.

The businesses most at risk of veil-piercing are those where the distinction between the owner and the entity has never been clearly established in practice. An LLC that has never held a member meeting, never adopted any resolutions, uses the owner's personal bank account for business receipts, and has never maintained separate books is functionally operating as though the entity does not exist, and a court may treat it that way.

For professional services businesses, technology companies, and any business that carries significant liability exposure, maintaining corporate formalities is not overhead. It is the mechanism that keeps owners' personal assets protected.

Governance and Outside Capital: What Investors Expect

The introduction of outside capital into a business, whether from angel investors, venture capital, private equity, or strategic partners, almost always triggers a fundamental change in the company's governance structure. Understanding what investors expect and how governance arrangements shift with outside capital helps founders negotiate from a position of knowledge rather than surprise.

Outside investors almost universally require preferred equity with rights that differ from common equity. Preferred equity typically carries liquidation preferences (the investor gets paid before common holders in a sale or liquidation), anti-dilution protections, and often participation rights that allow investors to share in sale proceeds after their preference is satisfied. These economic rights have governance implications: preferred investors frequently negotiate for board seats, consent rights over major decisions, and protective provisions that give them veto power over actions that would affect their investment.

The governance documents must be updated to reflect these arrangements accurately. A capitalization table that does not match the company's actual ownership structure, preference rights that are not properly documented, or board composition provisions that were never amended to add investor-designated seats all create problems that are difficult and expensive to unwind. Before completing any equity financing, the governance documents should be reviewed and amended by counsel to ensure they accurately reflect the post-financing structure.

If you are considering an equity raise or have recently completed one and are uncertain whether your governance documents are current, our corporate governance team can review your structure and identify any gaps before they become issues.

Starting Right: Building Governance Into Your Business From Day One

The ideal time to establish a strong governance framework is when the business is formed, before anyone is invested in a particular arrangement, before there are disputes about what was agreed upon, and before the complexity of the company's ownership structure makes documentation more difficult. The cost of drafting a thorough operating agreement or shareholder agreement and adopting properly structured governing documents is modest relative to the cost of reconstructing or litigating these arrangements later.

The most important governance questions to address at formation or shortly thereafter include: What is the ownership structure and what rights attach to each class of equity? Who has authority to bind the company to contracts and what dollar thresholds require board approval? How will decisions be made when co-owners disagree? What happens to a co-owner's interest if they leave, die, or become disabled? How will future equity be issued and at what price? What happens if the business needs additional capital and one co-owner is unwilling or unable to contribute?

These questions feel abstract when the relationship among co-founders is strong and the future looks uncomplicated. They become urgent when the relationship changes, when the business faces stress, or when a major transaction requires the governance structure to function under real pressure. The founders who build their governance structure when things are good are the ones who have it available to rely on when things get complicated.
